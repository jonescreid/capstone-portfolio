Two experiences shaped my first two years at Carolina more than any others. Inside the classroom, ITEC 101 changed my academic trajectory in ways I never anticipated when I signed up for it on a recommendation. Outside the classroom, becoming a Student Ambassador at the Darla Moore School of Business gave me a front-row seat to what it actually looks like to represent an institution with excellence - and then, eventually, to help shape what that excellence looks like for the next generation. On this page, I'll walk through both, and make the case that they're not as separate as they might seem.

A course I almost didn't take — and the one that stuck.

I enrolled in ITEC 101 because someone I trusted thought I'd find it interesting. That's it. No grand strategy, no calculated decision. I just said yes. What I didn't know was that saying yes to that class would rewrite part of my academic plan entirely.

ITEC 101 introduced me to the fundamentals of information technology - systems thinking, data structures, the architecture of how digital tools actually function beneath the interface. For a Finance and Supply Chain student, it clicked immediately. Operations is, at its core, an information problem. Supply chains live and die by data flow. The language of IT was already embedded in what I was studying; this course just gave me the vocabulary to name it.

What stood out most wasn't any single lecture - it was the quality of the learning environment. My professor brought a precision to the material that I hadn't encountered in many of my other courses. It was the kind of class where you left feeling like you'd actually been sharpened, not just informed. By the end of the semester, I had declared an Information Technology minor. That's not something I planned. That's something ITEC 101 earned.

The connection to my major and career goals became clearer the more I sat with it. Wealth management is increasingly a technology-informed field. Client data management, portfolio analytics, digital advisory tools - understanding the infrastructure behind those systems isn't a nice-to-have anymore. ITEC 101 gave me a head start on that fluency, and the minor I'm building out is a direct extension of what that course opened up.

03 — Beyond the Classroom

The job that felt like mine from the first tour.

I remember the ambassador who gave me a tour of the Moore School when I was still a prospective student. I don't remember every word they said, but I remember how I felt afterward - like this was somewhere I belonged. That feeling played a role in my college decision. So when I saw the application for the Ambassador Program in my first year, it felt less like an opportunity and more like a return.

I was selected in the spring of my freshman year as part of a highly selective cohort. The job itself is straightforward on the surface - lead tours, represent the school, field questions from families. But in practice, it's much more than that. Every conversation with a prospective student is a chance to tell the truth about what Moore is, and to do it with enough specificity and warmth that they leave with something real. I've led over 100 personalized tours. Each one is different.

This past spring, my role expanded significantly. I helped lead the interviewing process for the next class of ambassadors - narrowing a pool of 180+ applicants down to a selective cohort of 15. Since then, I've been promoted to assistant director, where I help with scheduling, coordinate events with faculty and staff, and play an active role in shaping the culture of the program going forward.

What makes this experience meaningful to me isn't the promotion or the resume line. It's the continuity. Someone did this for me, and now I get to do it for someone else. That loop - of being well-served and then turning around to serve well - is something I want to carry into every professional context I enter.

04 — Connection
ITEC 101 → Ambassador Program

Service isn't a soft skill.
It's a system.

On the surface, an IT course and a student ambassador role don't have much in common. But there's a course I took specifically to be eligible for the Masters Tournament - HTMT 591, a hospitality-focused class taught through the lens of five-star service - that sits at the center of both and pulls them together in an unexpected way.

HTMT 591 was about what it actually looks like to deliver exceptional service in a high-pressure, high-expectation environment. Augusta National isn't just a golf tournament. It's one of the most meticulously managed hospitality experiences in the world, and the standard of professionalism expected from every person on those grounds - from the competitors to the concession staff - is the same. I processed over 100 patrons per hour and $10K in daily sales during the tournament. That kind of environment has a way of clarifying what service at scale actually requires.

What I brought back to the Ambassador Program was a heightened attention to the experience of the person in front of me. Not just the information I'm conveying, but the feeling I'm creating. HTMT 591 made explicit something I had been doing intuitively - that every touchpoint matters, that consistency is the foundation of trust, and that the families I'm walking through the Moore School deserve the same level of care and intentionality as any five-star guest.

ITEC 101 added another layer. The systems thinking I developed in that course gave me a framework for understanding the Ambassador Program itself as a system - with inputs, processes, and outputs. When I stepped into the assistant director role and started shaping scheduling and culture, I was thinking about it the way an operations student would: where are the friction points, how do we standardize what works, and how do we build something that runs well even when I'm not in the room? The classroom gave me the mental models. The role gave me the place to use them.

That's the real connection. Not that these two experiences happened at the same time, but that each one made me better at the other - and together, they made me the kind of professional I'm trying to become.
